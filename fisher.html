About This Work

The Author

Mrs. Abby Fisher, formerly of Mobile, Alabama, is among the earliest known African American women to publish a cookbook in the United States. She produced this volume in 1881 at the direct request of patrons in San Francisco, Oakland, and Sacramento — women who had witnessed her skill firsthand and urged her to commit her knowledge to print.

In the preface she addresses, with candor and grace, the central difficulty: she could neither read nor write. Her husband, also without formal education, served as amanuensis, writing the book at her dictation. "After due consideration," she writes, "I concluded to bring forward a book of my knowledge — based on an experience of upwards of thirty-five years — in the art of cooking." The final entry, Recipe 160, closes with a quiet remark that has no parallel in nineteenth-century culinary literature: "I have given birth to eleven children and raised them all, and nursed them with this diet."

Historical Significance

Published fourteen years before Fannie Farmer's Boston Cooking-School Cook Book, What Mrs. Fisher Knows preserves a tradition of Southern Black cookery — gumbo, terrapin stew, Jumberlie (a forerunner of jambalaya), circuit hash, plantation corn bread — transmitted entirely through oral culture and professional practice.

Fisher's voice throughout is direct and authoritative. She seasons dishes "high," insists on fresh ingredients, adjusts heat across a wood-fired range, and notes where a recipe "is the most delicious in the cookery." The book is both practical instruction and cultural document.

Awards & Recognition

  • Two medals, San Francisco Mechanics' Institute Fair, 1880 — Best Pickles & Sauces and Best Assortment of Jellies & Preserves
  • Diploma, Sacramento State Fair, 1879
  • Blackberry Brandy (Recipe 101) took a diploma at the 1879 State Fair — the only recipe in the book to cite its own prize

The book was printed at the Women's Co-operative Printing Office, 420–430 Montgomery Street, San Francisco — itself a remarkable institution of the Gilded Age.

This Archive Entry

All 160 numbered recipes have been transcribed from the University of California copy, digitized by the Internet Archive with funding from Microsoft Corporation. Source scans are linked from each recipe.

The original printing contains no illustrations. Recipe numbering is Fisher's own. The text is reproduced faithfully, including her spelling of ochra, jumberlie, vigareets, and other period usages.
